Your BMI is your weight in kilograms divided by your height in meters squared. A BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is considered healthy by the World Health Organization. BMI is the most widely used screening tool for weight-related health risks. Doctors, insurers, and public health agencies around the world rely on it because it is quick, free, and requires nothing more than a scale and a tape measure. This guide explains exactly how BMI is calculated, what the categories mean, where the formula falls short, and how to use your results to make better health decisions.

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from your weight and height. Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet developed the formula in the 1830s, and it became a standard medical screening tool in the 20th century. BMI does not measure body fat directly. Instead, it uses your weight-to-height ratio as a proxy for body fatness at the population level.
